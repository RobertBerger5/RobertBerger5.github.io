Natural Selection

How did natural selection influence human evolution?


Climate Effects

Some scientists assume that several human adaptations, such as upright walking and tool making, were the result of only the savanna biome. However, Hominids lived in a wide variety of environments, so if this were true, then adaptations such as these would only be found in part of the population, not all of the species.

A more recent theory, variability selection, states that humans evolved not as a product of one climate, but as a result of instability in the climate over a period of time. During the evolutionary period, the environment and climate was ever changing as a result of temperature changes, precipitation changes, elevation changes due to plate tectonics, volcanic eruptions, and forest fires.

After a hominin’s environment changed, they either had to find a new environment similar to their old one or adapt to the changes in their current one, or else they would become extinct. Given the adaptation to varying climates, humans evolved in a very similar way, resulting in similar adaptations throughout the species. Also, those who were more adaptable survived changing environments and passed on their DNA. Natural selection was less about survival of the fittest, but survival of the most adaptable. Over time, the human race became much more adaptable, allowing them to survive and continue evolve.

One way in which hominids survived changing climates and environments was genetic adaptation. During this process, different alleles are present in varied frequencies among the population. Over time, one allele is favored, and through natural selection, becomes much more common among the population.

Another way in which organisms adapt in changing environments is to evolve new structures and behaviors to better help them survive their environment. The change over time in structures or behaviors as a reaction to an unstable environment is known as Variability Selection. Variability selection differs from hypotheses based on a consistent environment as it shows the survival of organisms who are better able to cope with change.

The adaptation of hominins to have two legs and long arms indicates instability during the period of evolution. The long limbs of hominins suggests that they needed to move along in varied habitats. With long legs and arms, they were able to move around quickly in diverse and changing environments.

The development of the ability of early hominins to use stone tools to access food suggests they adapted to changing and varied environments, as this allowed them to utilize a large number of food sources. The ability to make and use stone tools aided the function of the teeth and helped them to be able to consume many different types to food depending on what was available in their environment.

The ability of early hominids to adapt to their environment not only allowed them to survive in many different environments, but also to survive over other lineages. Species that weren’t able to adapt to a changing environment as quickly often went extinct, allowing the early ancestors of Homo Sapiens to survive and continue to evolve.

Survival of the Adaptable

The first humans appeared on the earth over 200,000 years ago. These homo sapiens were much different then we are today. Since they lacked the modern agriculture humans have today, they had to hunt and scavenge for food. Because of this hominins had to bring themselves closer to dangerous animals for food. Many human fossils have been found with bite marks in their bones. Those that were able to out run and out fight the predators survived, and passed on their traits.
