How does fossil evidence support human evolution?

As we look at fossil evidence we can see that our bone structures have changed over many years. For example the skull, pelvis, and leg bone have changed into what we are today. The skull to start off has grown bigger over time therefore enabling our brain to grown bigger too. Also the brain cavity size grew bigger as the teeth and jaw size decreased. Over time if we look at the capacity of the brain we can see that it started at 650 cubic centimeters for early humans and today it is about 1350 cubic centimeters. Comparing it to a chimpanzee which is roughly 300-500 cubic centimeters the modern brain today is three times bigger which would require the skull to accommodate to the proper size as we see the changes of the skull today.

Looking at the pelvic bone we can see that as chimpanzees learned to walk on their two feet their pelvic bone has formed into more of a bowl shape to hold their internal organs and body weight. The width of the pelvic bone also contributes to the length of the stride by rotating the pelvis while walking. As the rotation increases it would increase the length of the stride and decrease the allowance of swing in their step.

Attachment of the leg bone also played a key role in the shape of the pelvis as overtime the place where it attaches had changed due to vertical standing. Talking about leg bones the thigh bone enabled us to take longer strides which allowed us to walk farther and faster than earlier humans.

Lucy is the worlds most famous early human ancestor. She was an ape that was found 3.2 million years ago near Hadar in Ethiopia. Only 40% of her body was found but they figured out she was a female by the size of her body. When looking at her remains she had long dangly arms, but the rest of her body including the pelvis, spine, foot, and leg bone showed that she walked upright and stood 3.5 feet tall. Lucy was an adult female of about 25 years old.
